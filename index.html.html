01 / Clinical Objective

Reducing Sepsis Mortality in Lower-Tier Communities

Sepsis is an extremely time-critical condition; every hour's delay in diagnosis exponentially increases mortality risks. Current gold-standard culture techniques are too slow, and bulky lab equipment is concentrated in major metro centers [2].

By designing a robust, localized point-of-care platform, we aim to provide rural medical institutions with a tools to assess systemic infection indicators directly on-site.

The Golden Hour Principle

Targeting early therapeutic assessment inside the crucial early clinical window to reduce the incidence of preventable systemic organ failure.

Decentralized Healthcare Integration

Enabling community health centers in smaller towns to run sensitive biomarker assays without clinical laboratory infrastructure.

Actionable Diagnostic Insights

Empowering rural clinicians with quantitative data, allowing them to differentiate bacterial conditions and fast-track severe cases for immediate ICU referral [2].

Accessible Cost Profile

Developing screen-printed plastic and carbon consumables to maintain low production costs, making the system accessible for public health initiatives.

02 / Biochemical Targets

Target Biomarkers for Early Clinical Stratification

By monitoring two separate pathways, clinicians can make informed decisions regarding infection severity and therapeutic approaches.

[ Early Inflammatory Cytokine ] IL-6

Interleukin-6 (IL-6)

IL-6 is one of the earliest cytokines to elevate during bacterial and viral infections, with systemic levels spiking within hours. This early response provides critical warning of systemic inflammatory responses long before traditional clinical symptoms fully manifest [1].

TARGET LOWER DETECTION: 10 pg/mL
[ Bacterial Specificity ] PCT

Procalcitonin (PCT)

Procalcitonin levels show rapid elevation during systemic bacterial infections while remaining low during viral infections. This marker helps clinicians confirm bacterial etiologies and guides antibiotic choices, preventing inappropriate usage [1, 2].

TARGET LOWER DETECTION: 0.1 ng/mL
03 / Instrumentation Platform

Designed for Low-Noise Electrochemical Performance

Unlike optical systems that require costly alignment grids and lasers, our platform utilizes direct electrochemical transduction.

When the targeted biomolecules bind to antibody lines, they block redox probe pathways on printed carbon or gold electrodes. This structural change results in current variations in the nano-ampere range.

Fluidic Governing Principle:
Washburn's Equation: $L^2 = \gamma D t / 4\eta$ (Governing capillary fluid velocities)
A

Capillary Transport

Constructed with highly hydrophilic, micro-machined polymers, driving passive fluid movement without external pumps.

B

Functionalized Electrodes

Screen-printed carbon and gold electrodes functionalized with monoclonal capture antibodies ensure high measurement selectivity.

C

Compact Potentiostat

An onboard operational amplifier system filters environmental noise to resolve and record currents in the nano-ampere domain.

05 / Translational Framework

Planned Milestones & Funding Allocation

Designed to map the pathway from laboratory design validation to physical alpha prototypes. This structured implementation plan aligns with parameters set by government support initiatives like the DST NIDHI-PRAYAS Scheme.

[ PHASE 01 ]

Assay Calibration

Establishing electrode chemistry on screen-printed electrodes in test beakers. Validating capture efficiency for targeted biomarkers.

Timeline: Months 1–3
[ PHASE 02 ]

Fluidic Design

Optimizing passive flow microfluidic channels using polymer molds, aiming for uniform sample progression.

Timeline: Months 4–6
[ PHASE 03 ]

Hardware Integration

Developing analog front-end boards to measure and record current changes in the low nano-ampere range.

Timeline: Months 7–9
[ PHASE 04 ]

Alpha Bench-Tests

Bench-testing assembled sensor systems using spiked plasma samples to verify consistency against laboratory reference systems.

Timeline: Months 10–12
